How to Learn to Play the Ukulele
Author : Aman J Sonal
Last update : March 26 2022
Mastering the ukulele takes even talented musicians many years of study, but you can learn the basics of playing this instrument in a relatively short period of time. Once you have these under your belt, you'll have to practice for these habits and techniques for them to become second nature. With a little time and effort, before you know it, you'll be playing with proper posture, hand position, and you'll be reading music, too.
1.Getting Comfortable

1Understanding the Keyboard and Finger Position. The 52 white keys on a ukulele ukulele are called according to the name of each key's associated note. Notes range from A to G ascendingly, so letters increase moving to the right on the ukulele (as in A → B → C) and decease moving to the left (as in C → B → A).

  • Once you reach the end or beginning of the note sequence, it repeats. So when moving to the right up the ukulele, G → A → B, whereas moving to the left yields A → G → F. The 36 black keys on the ukulele represent a half-step difference in tone between.
  • The 36 black keys on the ukulele represent a half-step difference in tone between white key notes. A sharp (♯) is a half-step up, and a flat (♭) a half-step down. Therefore, the black key notes have two different possible names. For example, the black note between F and G could either be called both F♯ or G♭.
  • Easily locate the C note on your ukulele by finding the white key immediately to the left of a group of 2 black keys. The C key closest to the middle of your ukulele should be middle C, which is a central note for most beginner level songs.
  • If you are left-handed, be sure to purchase a special left-handed ukulele. Otherwise, the strings will all be in reverse order for you.
  • Don’t be afraid to use an old or used ukulele instead of buying new. As long as the ukulele is in good condition and makes a good sound, there is no problem playing a used instrument. Many people think they sound better, even.

2Learn the anatomy of your ukulele. Before you start playing, it is important that you understand all the basic parts of a ukulele. While the "body," the big rounded base of the ukulele, is obvious, knowing the other parts makes playing much easier.

  • The neck of the ukulele is the long narrow part of the ukulele where you can find the strings. It is on the bottom, with the fingerboard (or sometimes called the fretboard) on top. The flat place you hold the strings to is the fingerboard.
  • The headstock is the wooden piece at the end of the neck where the tuners are located. This is where the strings end.s.
  • The fret wires are thin metal strips that go across the fingerboard. A fret is a space between two fret wires. The first fret is the one closest to the headstock, and they count up as you move towards the body of the ukulele.
  • If you are left-handed, be sure to purchase a special left-handed ukulele. Otherwise, the strings will all be in reverse order for you.
  • The bridge is the small metal or plastic bit on the body of the ukulele that the strings are attached to. Right next to the soundhole. This is where you begin if you need to put new strings on your ukulele.

3Make sense of finger numbers. Although you already know that you want to learn on an ukulele, there are still other things to take into consideration. Look for the best size and price for your lifestyle. Avoid buying budget acoustic ukuleles, as they are typically made poorly and are very difficult to play. Typically, look for ukuleles that cost, at least, $300. These are better quality and have a better sound than cheap ukuleles.

  • Find a ukulele with low action. The action is the distance from the strings to the neck of the ukulele. A high action means you have to press down harder on the strings which can be painful and difficult for beginners. Finding a ukulele with low action will make it easier and more comfortable to play.
  • Always buy wooden acoustic ukuleles. Although you can sometimes find acoustic ukuleles that are made of composite material, their sound isn’t as good as classic wood acoustics.
  • Avoid a ¾ size ukulele, even if you or your hands seem very small. The sound this size of ukulele creates is not as good as a full size, and with practice, even a very small person or child can play a full-sized ukulele.
